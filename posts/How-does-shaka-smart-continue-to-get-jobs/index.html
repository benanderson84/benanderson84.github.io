How does Shaka Smart continue to get jobs?

basketball
college
coaches
Author

Ben Anderson

Published

April 14, 2025

As the landscape of college basketball has changed because of the transfer portal and NIL, an aspect that fans have lost in the NCAA Tournament is the Cinderella teams that really bring the tournament alive.

One of the more notable Cinderella teams that come to mind is the 2011 VCU Rams coached by Shaka Smart. Smart’s team surpassed all expectations and made it to the Final Four before the season was ended by Gordon Hayward and the Butler Bulldogs.

After just one Sweet Sixteen appearance following that run including seven losses in the Round of 64 and two other career stops at Texas and now Marquette, Shaka has continued to be unsuccessful at the most prestigious tournament in college basketball. After another first-round exit in the postseason, it begs the question: How does Shaka Smart keep getting coaching jobs?

Let’s find out.

Sports Reference provides box scores of every college basketball game from 2010-2025 representing when Smart was hired by VCU in 2010 after the departure of Anthony Grant.

Let’s take a look at the number of wins at the three different schools where Smart has coached and compare them to how each program performed during his different tenures.

Code 
library(ggplot2)
library(tidyverse)

boxscores <- read_csv("boxscores20102025.csv")

totalpoints <- boxscores |> 
  group_by(season, team_display_name) |> 
  summarize(
    total_games = n(),
    total_points_scored = sum(team_score),
    total_points_allowed = sum(opponent_team_score)
  ) |> 
  filter(total_points_scored > 300) |> 
  mutate(
    season_ppg = total_points_scored / total_games,
    season_dppg = total_points_allowed / total_games
  ) |> 
  mutate(team_season = paste(team_display_name, season))

winning <- boxscores |> 
  mutate(Win = case_when(team_score > opponent_team_score ~ 1, team_score < opponent_team_score ~ 0)) |> 
  group_by(team_display_name, season) |> 
  summarize(
    games = n(),
    wins = sum(Win)
  ) |> 
  mutate(team_season = paste(team_display_name, season))

shakateamwinning <- winning |> 
  filter(team_display_name == "VCU Rams" | team_display_name == "Texas Longhorns" | team_display_name == "Marquette Golden Eagles" )

shakavcu <- shakateamwinning |> filter(team_display_name == "VCU Rams") |> filter(season >= 2010 & season <= 2015)
shakatex <- shakateamwinning |> filter(team_display_name == "Texas Longhorns") |> filter(season >= 2016 & season <= 2021) 
shakamar <- shakateamwinning |> filter(team_display_name == "Marquette Golden Eagles") |> filter(season >= 2022 & season <= 2025)

  ggplot() + 
  geom_line(data=shakateamwinning, aes(x=season, y=wins, group = team_display_name), color="grey") +
  geom_line(data=shakavcu, aes(x=season, y=wins, group = team_display_name), color="#FFB300", linewidth=2) +  
  geom_line(data=shakatex, aes(x=season, y=wins, group = team_display_name), color="#bf5700", linewidth=2) +  
  geom_line(data=shakamar, aes(x=season, y=wins, group = team_display_name), color="#003366", linewidth=2) +
  labs(
    x="Season", 
    y="Number of Wins", 
    title="Shaka's Slippery Slope", 
    subtitle="How did Shaka Smart impact these programs during his tenure?", 
    caption="Source: Basketball Reference | By Ben Anderson"
    ) + 
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(size = 20, face = "bold"),
    axis.title = element_text(size = 10), 
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size=13), 
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
    plot.title.position = "plot",
    )

All three programs showed a decrease during Smart’s tenure with the school, including a Texas program that managed only 11 wins in the 2017 season. When he settled in a power conference such as the Big 12, Smart’s number of wins in a season settled down to around 20 wins. After he departed for Marquette, the number of wins for Texas shot back up to that 25-win mark where they are joined by another one of Smart’s former employers, VCU.

With multiple seasons of 25+ wins, it is difficult to appreciate only one Sweet Sixteen appearance that featured his team that was given a number two seed in the tournament. Let’s put his Final Four season into perspective against previous NCAA Champions over the years.

Granted, a team like VCU will most likely not match up with other blue bloods like Duke and Kentucky. But how about Shaka’s team that knocked off No. 1 seeded Kansas? They sit towards the bottom compared to every team he has coached in his career.

The 2011 run looks like it truly was one of the great Cinderella stories in recent years. Let’s take a closer look at how his team has performed offensively in the Big Dance in comparison to the historic run with VCU.

Code 
postseason <- boxscores |> 
  filter(season_type == "3")


postseasonfg <- postseason |> 
  mutate(
    team_season = paste(team_display_name, season)

Smart has continued to fail when it matters most, losing to a lower seed in eight of his last nine NCAA tournament appearances. That is not easy on anybody.

College athletics has a current focus on constant movement and that fits in perfectly with the rollercoaster coaching career Shaka Smart has had in his sixteen-year career. Players and coaches are moving around and opportunities are opening up for people everywhere.

Is Shaka Smart riding the high of the 2011 VCU season as he moves around to these different opportunities? It certainly is not because of his performance in the postseason.
